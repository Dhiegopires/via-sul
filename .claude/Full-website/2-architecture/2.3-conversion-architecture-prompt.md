# Conversion Architecture: AI Execution Prompt

You are generating the conversion architecture document for a VellumWire client. This runs after the sitemap (2.1) and user journeys (2.2), before design starts. Your task: lock the persuasion sequence, CTA placement rules, and attention budget for every priority page, so that section order stops being a build-time improvisation and becomes an approved, traceable decision.

**Why this step exists and why it runs here, not later:**
A page's conversion rate is decided by its structure before a single word of copy or pixel of design exists. If the section order and CTA placement are left as a loose list ("sections this page must contain"), the AI building the page in Step 5 has no way to know which order actually moves a visitor toward a decision — it will sequence sections by what reads well in isolation, not by what a real visitor needs to see, in what order, to say yes. By the time this shows up as a symptom (a CTA buried mid-page, a visitor who never crosses a real decision point), the page is built, approved, and often already spending traffic. Fixing structure after launch is fixing a foundation after the house is up. This step makes the structure a client-approved, locked decision at the same point the sitemap and journeys are locked — before Step 3 (design) ever starts.

This document, once approved, supersedes the free-form "sections this page must contain" list from Step 2.1. If they conflict, this document wins, and 2.1 should be corrected to match it.

## WHAT TO PRODUCE

### Part 1: Awareness level per page

For each page, using the persona's Trigger (what they typed, what state they're in) and funnel stage from 2.2, classify the primary visitor's awareness level using Eugene Schwartz's five stages:

- **Unaware**: doesn't know they have the problem
- **Problem-aware**: knows the problem, doesn't know solutions exist
- **Solution-aware**: knows solutions exist, hasn't found this one
- **Product-aware**: knows this product, hasn't decided
- **Most-aware**: ready, just needs the offer and the friction removed

This classification is not decorative. It determines how much agitation the page owes the visitor before it's allowed to ask for anything. A most-aware visitor (branded search, referral) can be asked immediately. A problem-aware visitor asked immediately is being asked a question their brain hasn't finished forming an opinion on yet — that's why the ask fails, not because the button is the wrong color.

### Part 2: Formula assignment per page

Assign exactly one persuasion formula per page from this fixed set. Do not invent a new one. Do not blend two on the same page.

- **PAS** (Problem — Agitate — Solve): best for problem-aware or unaware visitors who need the pain named and intensified before the solution lands.
- **BAB** (Before — After — Bridge): best when the contrast between current state and outcome state is the strongest lever available.
- **StoryBrand SB7** (Character has a Problem, meets a Guide, who gives them a Plan, calls them to Action, which helps them avoid Failure and reach Success): best for pages where trust in the provider (not just the mechanism) is the primary objection.
- **AIDA** (Attention — Interest — Desire — Action): best for solution-aware or product-aware visitors who already believe the category works and need this option differentiated fast.

State which formula each page uses and why, referencing the awareness level from Part 1 and the objection from Step 2.2. A most-aware, high-intent page (e.g. a pricing or demo page reached by product-aware traffic) may legitimately use AIDA with the CTA immediately visible. A cold, problem-aware landing page must not.

**Ground this against the competitor conversion structure, do not decide in a vacuum.** For each page, name what the analyzed competitors actually do structurally (from Step 1.3): if every competitor in this category runs a short PAS-style page with the CTA in section 2, and the client's persona and awareness level match theirs, that is evidence the category has already converged on what works — deviating from it needs a stated reason, not just a preference for a different framework. If competitors share a structural weakness (CTA buried past section 5, attention ratio of 6:1 or worse), that is the structural opportunity from Step 1.3's Opportunity Map, and this page should deliberately do the opposite. Either way, say explicitly which competitor(s) informed this page's formula choice, or state that no competitor data was available and flag `[DATA NEEDED: competitor conversion structure]`.

### Part 3: Mandatory section sequence

For each page, output the exact ordered list of sections. Every section carries three labels:

- **Formula stage**: which stage of the assigned formula this section fulfills (e.g. "Agitate" for PAS, "Guide" for StoryBrand)
- **Conversion lever** (MECLABS/MarketingExperiments Conversion Sequence Heuristic, Flint McGlaughlin — a prioritization checklist, not a literal equation to compute): which single variable this section exists to move — **m**otivation of the visitor (given, not created by the page), force of the **v**alue proposition, **i**ncentive that offsets unavoidable friction, **f**riction (reduce it), or **a**nxiety about the offer or the process (reduce it). One lever per section. If a section pulls zero levers, cut it — it's decoration. If it tries to pull three, split it into two sections.
- **Internal link note**: which other page this section sends to or receives from, per Step 2's linking structure

This list is what Step 4.3 (layout preview) validates against, and what Step 5 (Claude Code) builds from. It is not a suggestion — reordering it after approval requires re-running this step, not a silent fix during development.

### Part 4: CTA placement rules

For each page, define:

- **Primary CTA visibility, mobile-first**: the primary CTA must be visible without scrolling at a 375px mobile viewport — check this first; it is the primary case, not an afterthought (mobile is just over half of global web traffic — StatCounter and DataReportal both put it around 51-52% as of 2026 — and it's the only version Google ranks by: mobile-first indexing has been 100% of all sites since July 2024, confirmed on Google's own Search Central blog). Confirm it holds at a common desktop viewport too, but a page that only satisfies the fold check on desktop does not pass. Name the exact CTA text and the section it lives in. If the hero has any JS-driven default state (a carousel, a segment/audience picker, a tab switcher), this rule applies to whatever renders first, automatically, before any visitor interaction — not to a state the visitor might reach after clicking something. A CTA that only appears after the visitor manually navigates a carousel does not satisfy this rule.
- **If the page's awareness level requires agitation before the primary ask** (unaware or problem-aware personas): define a lower-commitment secondary CTA that occupies that same above-the-fold position instead (e.g. "See how it works" instead of "Buy now"), and state explicitly which later section is the first one allowed to carry the primary, high-commitment CTA. The secondary CTA does not have to be a full page or a full contact form — a short, low-field inline capture (name + contact only, no qualification questions) is a legitimate lower-commitment path when the primary conversion mechanism is an external scheduling tool the client wants to keep (e.g. Calendly) and the client does not want a full form. What matters is that the secondary CTA is real and built, not that it's elaborate.
- **Link honesty, non-negotiable**: whatever text names the destination of a CTA (primary or secondary) must be the actual destination. If a secondary CTA is offered as an alternative to the primary one ("prefer to do X instead?"), it must lead somewhere genuinely different that does X — never the same href as the primary CTA with different wording on top, and never a page that doesn't exist yet. State the exact destination for every CTA named in this document so Step 5 has no ambiguity to fill in with a shortcut.
- **CTA repetition cadence**: the maximum number of consecutive sections allowed to pass with no visible next step. Default rule: no more than 3 sections in a row without a CTA (primary or secondary) — a visitor should never have to hunt for what to do next.

### Part 5: Attention ratio budget

Count every clickable action that is not the primary CTA and that appears above the fold or within the mandated section sequence: nav items, secondary CTAs, footer teasers pulled into view, unrelated outbound links. State the count and the ratio against the single primary goal (Unbounce's attention ratio: a true landing page targets 1:1; a marketing site homepage will never hit 1:1 because of navigation, but no single competing action should out-weigh the primary CTA visually or in copy prominence). Flag explicitly if the count suggests the primary CTA is competing with more than 3 other above-the-fold actions. State how this ratio compares to the competitor ratios gathered in Step 1.3 — matching a weak category norm is not a defense.

### Part 6: Drop-off cross-reference

For each page, restate the persona's drop-off risk and objection from Step 2.2, and name the exact section in the mandated sequence that addresses it. If no section addresses a named drop-off risk, that is a gap — flag it, do not silently leave it unaddressed.

## TONE RULES

- Every section must have a stated lever. A section with no lever is a section with no reason to exist.
- Do not invent an awareness level or objection that isn't traceable to Step 2.2. If a persona's journey doesn't give you enough to classify awareness, flag `[DATA NEEDED]` rather than guessing.
- One formula per page. Mixing formulas mid-page is how sections end up contradicting each other's pacing.
- No AI language anywhere in the document.

## CRITICAL AI GUARDRAILS (HALLUCINATION-PROOFING)

To prevent AI hallucinations and generic "slop" output, you must strictly follow these rules:
1. **No Invented Personas Data**: Awareness level, objections, and drop-off risk must trace directly to Step 2.2. Do not invent psychographic detail not present in the input.
2. **No Framework Invention**: Only use PAS, BAB, StoryBrand SB7, or AIDA. Do not create a hybrid or a new named framework.
3. **No Invented Competitor Structure**: Every claim about what a competitor does structurally (section order, CTA position, attention ratio) must trace to Step 1.3's input. If Step 1.3 did not capture conversion structure for a competitor, do not infer or guess it here — write `[DATA NEEDED: competitor conversion structure not captured in Step 1.3]` and go back to Step 1.3 rather than inventing a plausible layout.
4. **No AI Copy Patterns**:
   - Do not use em dashes (`—`) in copy.
   - Do not use emojis in written body text or headlines.
   - Do not use SaaS clichés ("Not only X, but Y", "Transform your business").
