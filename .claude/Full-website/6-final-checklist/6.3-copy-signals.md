# Step 6.3 — Copy Signals Reference

Manual reference for thorough mode. Use after running `6.2-audit-prompt.md`. Each item maps to a checklist ID (C1–C20).

---

### C1 — "Not X, but Y" contrast trap
One contrast pair per page is acceptable. Two or more means the AI was filling space. Fix: keep the strongest one, cut the rest. A contrast without specifics on the positive side is empty.

### C2 — Short sentence punch overuse
Effective once per section. Filler at twice. A pattern at three. Fix: one per section. The other belongs in body copy, not as a standalone beat.

### C3 — Repeated statistics
List every number and percentage on the page. Any appearing more than once is wallpaper, not evidence. Fix: each number appears once, in the section where it has the most argumentative weight.

### C4 — Repeated key phrases
After reading the full page, list phrases that stuck. If any appear more than once across different sections, the copy ran out of specific things to say. Fix: one appearance per phrase. If worth repeating, it belongs only in the headline.

### C5 — "That's by design" and equivalents
Exact phrases to flag and delete: "That's by design." / "And that's intentional." / "This is a deliberate choice." / "Not by accident." / "By design, not default." Fix: delete. If the decision was good, the result demonstrates it.

### C6 — Meta-commentary about the work
Sentences describing what a section is trying to do instead of doing it. Symptoms: "Much of the work in this phase lives in..." / "This is where the product explains..." / "The goal of this section is to establish..." Fix: cut the sentence, start where the content starts.

### C7 — Generic filler phrases
Phrases that occupy space without adding information. Any competitor could paste them unchanged:
- close the loop / without overwhelming the user / straightforward path forward / fits into existing workflows
- make informed decisions / actionable insights / seamless experience / at every breakpoint
- end to end / first sketch to final pixel / full design surface / solo or embedded in product teams
- details that make products feel alive / built to scale without breaking / work as hard as they look

Fix: delete. If the sentence collapses without the phrase, rewrite with specific language.

### C8 — Three-item adjective lists
Exactly three generic adjectives for the same thing. "Fast, flexible, and reliable." Any triple equally true on a competitor's site. Fix: one specific adjective beats three generic ones.

### C9 — Defensive framing
Preemptive disclaimers that undercut the claim: "These patterns aren't invented here." / "This isn't a novel concept." / "We're not claiming to have solved X." / "Obviously this won't work for every team." Fix: cut the disclaimer, fix the claim.

### C10 — Generic CTAs
Button text that could appear on any site: "Discover More" / "Start Your Journey" / "Optimize Now" / "Transform Your Business" / "Explore the Possibilities" / "Get Started" / "Learn More." Fix: the CTA states what happens next. "Book a 15-min call" is specific. "Start Your Journey" is fiction.

### C11 — Soulless institutional copy
Company descriptions where swapping the name for a competitor's leaves the paragraph intact. Common subtypes:
- Portfolio clichés: "end to end product design" / "from research to final handoff" / "working closely with PMs, engineers, and stakeholders"
- Executive jargon: "operating layer for ongoing client delivery" / "cross-functional alignment" / "from zero to shelf-ready"
- Role inflation: "Led end-to-end product design across SaaS, fintech, and B2B." — could describe any designer anywhere

Fix: the copy must contain at least one specific claim that becomes false if the company name changes.

### C12 — Headline/subheadline disconnect
Read only the headline. What question does it create? Read only the subheadline. Does it answer that question? If you can swap the subheadline with any other on the page and nothing breaks, they're disconnected. Fix: write them as a unit — headline creates tension, subheadline resolves or proves it.

### C13 — Fragmented headline poetry
Headlines split across 3+ lines with deliberate breaks to simulate dramatic rhythm. "Screens that work / as hard as / they look." Read as a continuous sentence: if it sounds like a tagline any agency could publish, the breaks are covering a weak claim. Fix: write a headline strong enough for one line, or cut it.

### C14 — Content duplication as depth
Same services/features appearing twice on the same page in different visual formats (list → expanded sections, overview grid → services section) where the second pass contains no new information. Fix: each piece of information appears once. Expanded sections must contain information not in the summary.

### C15 — Generic testimonials
Quotes that could have been written without the reviewer using the product. "The team was incredibly responsive and the results exceeded our expectations." No specific feature, no actual number. Fix: if no specific quote exists, write `[TESTIMONIAL NEEDED: ask for a quote mentioning [specific feature] and a measurable result]`. A generic quote does more damage than no quote.

### C16 — Em dashes in copy
The em dash (`—`) is statistically the most common punctuation in AI-generated text. Every instance is a signal. Fix: replace with a period, colon, comma, or parenthesis. No exceptions. Grep before launch.

### C17 — Emojis in body copy
Emojis in headlines, body text, or CTAs substitute for specific language. Fix: cut every emoji from copy. If the sentence loses meaning without it, rewrite with words.

### C18 — Placeholder text
Blockers: "lorem ipsum" / "[CLIENT NAME]" / "[company]" / "insert your text" / "placeholder" / "coming soon" where real content was expected. Fix: nothing ships with unfilled fields.

### C19 — Link text/destination mismatch (Blocker)
The visible text of a link or button promises one destination or action, but the `href` goes somewhere else — most commonly a secondary "lower commitment" link silently pointing to the exact same destination as the primary CTA next to it. Example found in production: "Prefer to send a message? Access the contact page" with `href="https://calendly.com/..."` — the same external scheduling link as the primary button, while a real `/contato/` page didn't exist. Fix: either build the promised destination, or rewrite the link text to say what actually happens when it's clicked. Never let the two silently diverge — a visitor who clicks expecting one thing and lands on another loses trust in every other claim on the page, and that loss shows up as abandonment, not as a bug report.

### C20 — Proof that doesn't answer "will this make me money"
A number can be real, non-fabricated, and still be the wrong thing to lead with. Before any stat goes into a results/proof section, ask what it tells a skimming, non-technical reader (a business owner deciding whether to hire this agency) about their own outcome — not whether the number is accurate. Symptoms: Lighthouse score, page-load time, time-on-page, "1 CTA throughout the page," clicks-per-step-of-a-form headlining a results section, while an available lead/conversion number (form submissions, qualified consultations, "X leads became Y clients") sits second, buried, or omitted. A related failure mode: when told to move proof "to the top" or "above the fold," moving only the single most impressive number into the hero while the full results section stays wherever it was — this satisfies the letter of the instruction, not the substance. Confirmed production example: a case study's primary results grid led with Lighthouse score and time-on-page while the actual lead-generation number sat second in a three-column row, and a separate results section with real conversion data still lived roughly 70% down the page after a first-pass "move it up" edit only duplicated one stat into the hero. Fix: rank every candidate stat by how directly it answers "will I get more customers," lead with the top 1–3, cut or demote the rest to a small footnote line, and when relocating a section, confirm the whole block moved — grep the page structure, don't just eyeball the hero.
