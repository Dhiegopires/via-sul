# Market & Competitor Research: AI Execution Prompt

You are generating a market and competitor research report for a VellumWire client. The output is a structured `.md` file saved in the client project. Your task: conduct thorough market and competitor research using web search and document all findings with precision.

Run this prompt in parallel with the client form. You do not need to wait for all answers to start researching.

---

## CLIENT CONTEXT

Fill these fields before running:

- Company: [NAME]
- Industry: [INDUSTRY/NICHE]
- Product/service: [WHAT THEY DO]
- Ideal client: [WHO BUYS]
- Operating region: [WHERE THEY OPERATE]
- Competitors mentioned by client: [LIST: leave blank if none provided]

---

## WHAT TO RESEARCH

Search the web for each section. Flag clearly when something is an estimate vs. a verified fact. Be specific: cite real URLs, real H1s, real keywords found on competitor sites. No generic statements.

**Part 1: Market overview**
How do people search for this product/service on Google? List the most common search patterns: different intents, language variations, frequent questions. What is the maturity level of this market (emerging niche vs. saturated)? What is the main objection buyers have before converting? Is there any seasonality or regulatory urgency?

**Part 2: Competitor analysis**
Find a minimum of 3 competitors (include those mentioned by the client plus others found through research). For each:
- Name and URL
- Value proposition communicated: what does the H1 actually say?
- Communication strengths: what do they do well in their site and copy?
- Weaknesses: what is missing, wrong, or generic?
- Visual direction: dark or light, dominant colors, style feel
- Keywords they appear to be targeting based on site content and page titles
- Main CTA: what do they ask the visitor to do?
- **Conversion structure (this feeds Step 2.3 directly — do not skip)**: on their primary page (usually homepage), list the section order top to bottom as they actually appear (e.g. "Hero, Logos bar, 3-step process, Testimonial, Pricing, FAQ, Final CTA"). State plainly which section carries the first CTA, and whether that CTA is visible without scrolling on a common desktop viewport. Count roughly how many other links/CTAs compete with the main CTA within that first viewport (nav items, secondary buttons).

**Part 3: Opportunity map**
What is no competitor communicating but should be? What is the strongest positioning angle available for the client ("the only one focused on X", "faster than Y", "science-backed when everyone else is generic")? What visual direction is available to differentiate? If every competitor is light and blue, going dark is a differentiator. Which keywords are competitors targeting where the client has a realistic chance of winning?

**Structural opportunity (feeds Step 2.3):** Looking at the conversion structure gathered in Part 2 across all competitors, is there a shared structural weakness — every competitor burying the CTA past 3+ sections, no competitor repeating the CTA, every competitor running an attention ratio of 5:1 or worse in their hero? A page that fixes a structural failure the whole category shares is a real, defensible advantage, not just a copy angle. If competitors already do this well, say so explicitly instead of inventing a gap that isn't there.

**Part 4: Preliminary keyword map**
Minimum 20 keywords organized into clusters:
- Cluster 1: product/solution (what the client sells, commercial intent)
- Cluster 2: client pain (problem they search to solve, informational intent)
- Cluster 3: comparison/alternative (vs. competitor, alternative to X, transactional intent)
- Cluster 4: long-tail/blog (informational questions for content strategy)
- Cluster 5: urgency/regulation (if applicable to this market)

For each keyword: estimated volume (high/medium/low), intent, difficulty (high/medium/low), recommended page.

## OUTPUT FORMAT

Gere um arquivo `.md` salvo como `pesquisa-mercado-[cliente].md` na pasta do projeto. Seções em ordem:

1. **Cabeçalho**: nome do cliente, data, concorrentes analisados, maturidade do mercado
2. **Visão Geral do Mercado**: 4 findings — cada um com título, dado real, implicação para o cliente
3. **Análise de Concorrentes**: sub-seção por concorrente — nome, URL, H1 real do site, pontos fortes, fraquezas, oportunidade de diferenciação, e **estrutura de conversão** (ordem real das seções, seção onde aparece o primeiro CTA, se esse CTA é visível sem rolar, contagem aproximada de ações concorrentes na primeira viewport)
4. **Mapa de Oportunidades**: 3–4 oportunidades com título e justificativa baseada nos dados coletados, incluindo a oportunidade estrutural (Parte 3) se houver uma fraqueza estrutural compartilhada entre concorrentes
5. **Mapa de Keywords**: clusters — cada cluster com label, palavras-chave, volume estimado (marcar como estimativa), dificuldade, intenção de busca

Para dados ausentes: `> ⚠️ [DATA NEEDED: descreva o que falta]`

## TONE RULES

- Direct: name the finding without softening it
- Specific: cite real H1s, real URLs, real keywords. Never generalize.
- No AI language: never "crucial", "worth noting", "landscape", "leverage"
- Confident: state what the data shows, state the recommendation
- Flag estimates: when a keyword volume or difficulty is estimated, say so explicitly

---

## CRITICAL AI GUARDRAILS (HALLUCINATION-PROOFING)

To prevent AI hallucinations and generic "slop" output, you must strictly follow these rules:
1. **Strict Data Grounding**: Do not invent/hallucinate any competitor sites, H1 text, or keywords. Every competitor mentioned must be a real company with an actual web page. Cite real H1s found on their live sites. The same applies to conversion structure: only report a section order or CTA position you actually observed on the live page. If you cannot access the page well enough to determine this, write `[DATA NEEDED: could not verify conversion structure for this competitor]` instead of guessing a plausible-sounding layout.
2. **Keyword Integrity**: All keywords in the keyword map must be real search queries. Flag estimates on volume/difficulty explicitly.
3. **No Dummy Content**: If any context or competitor list is missing, write `[DATA NEEDED: describe what's missing]` styled in danger color. Do not invent details.
4. **No AI Copy Patterns**:
   - Do not use em dashes (`—`) in copy.
   - Do not use emojis in written body text or headlines.
   - Do not use SaaS clichés ("Not only X, but Y", "Transform your business").
5. **Cross-Source Verification (not just anti-invention)**: rules 1-3 stop you from making things up. This rule stops you from repeating something real that's wrong. Any market-size, consumer-behavior, ranking-factor, or pricing/benchmark statistic that will inform a strategic decision (positioning, pricing, what to claim in client-facing copy) must be corroborated by 2+ independent sources before being stated as fact — not just cited once and moved on from.
   - **Primary vs. secondary:** a page that conducted the original research (a named survey, a documented test) outranks a page that merely cites or repeats a number from elsewhere. If you can only find the number on secondary/aggregator pages, say so explicitly rather than presenting it with the confidence of a primary source.
   - **Commercial bias:** discount or flag a stat sourced from a vendor whose own product/course/service benefits from the reader believing it (a tool's blog claiming a pain point only their tool solves, a course provider's stat about how urgent that course's skill is). Look for the same number from a source with nothing to sell before treating it as settled.
   - **Single-source numbers get marked, not hidden:** if a specific figure only traces to one source anywhere you can find, state it as `[SINGLE SOURCE, unverified: figure + source name]` rather than dropping the caveat once the number "sounds right."
   - Confirmed production example: a separate internal research pass on a Google Business Profile management plan found a cited "42%/28%/17% suspension-cause breakdown" that does not exist anywhere in the named source's real published material, a "$X per suspension" revenue-loss estimate traceable to a single small blog, and a badge name (the LSA "Google Guaranteed" trust badge) that had been retired months before the document was written. None of these were invented from nothing, which is why rules 1-3 alone wouldn't have caught them — they were real-sounding claims that just hadn't been checked against a second source.
