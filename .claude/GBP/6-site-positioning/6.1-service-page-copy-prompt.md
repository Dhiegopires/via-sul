## The one rule that overrides everything else in this file

**The target reader is a small business owner who often does not know what "Google Business Profile" is, let alone the acronym "GBP."** They know their business shows up (or doesn't) when someone searches for it on Google or Google Maps. Every sentence on this page has to work for that reader on first read, with zero acronyms and zero assumed vocabulary.

```
NEVER USE, anywhere in client-facing copy:
- "GBP" (the acronym)
- "Google Business Profile" as if it's a known term (fine to name it once, in
  passing, the way you'd name a legal term — never lead with it or rely on it)
- "local SEO", "Map Pack", "NAP consistency", "citations", "prominence signals"
- any term from this internal methodology folder used verbatim in client copy

INSTEAD SAY:
- "your Google listing" / "your profile that shows up on Google Maps"
- "when someone searches for [service] near them"
- "the three results Google shows at the top of a map search" (instead of "Map Pack")
- "making sure your name, address, and phone number match everywhere online"
  (instead of "NAP consistency")
```

If a sentence needs a acronym or piece of jargon to make sense, the sentence is wrong, not the reader.

## Reworked positioning (adapted from the internal masterplan, verified data only)

**Tagline direction:** "We put your business in front of people who are already looking for you." Works because it covers both this service (showing up) and VellumWire's existing website work (converting once found) without assuming the reader knows either is a distinct technical discipline.

**The core claim that's actually confirmed and safe to say:** most small businesses never finish setting up their Google listing properly (over half haven't even verified it, per Searchlab/SOCi 2026 data), and an active, complete, well-photographed profile gets dramatically more calls and direction requests than a bare-bones one (BrightLocal's own photo study found over 500% more calls on profiles with 100+ photos vs. sparse ones — cite the direction of the effect, not the exact old percentage, in client-facing copy).

**What NOT to claim:** a specific lead count, a specific ranking position, or "Google is the only place customers check" (BrightLocal's own 2026 data shows Google's share of local-business research actually fell to 71% in 2026 from 83% the year before — still the largest single channel, but say "the place most people check first," not "the only place that matters").

## Prompt

```
Write the vellumwire.com service page copy for a new offering: setting up and
managing a small business's Google listing so it shows up when local customers
search.

Audience: a small business owner (plumber, electrician, HVAC, contractor-type
business) who likely does not know technical search-marketing terms. Zero
acronyms. Zero assumed vocabulary — explain every concept in the words a
customer would use, not the words an agency would use.

Structure (fill each section, following VellumWire's existing conversion
architecture discipline — see Full-website/2-architecture/2.3):

HERO
Headline: outcome-first, plain language (e.g. "More calls from people already
searching for you")
Sub: one sentence on what the service actually does, no jargon
Primary CTA: a free audit offer

PROBLEM SECTION
Name the real problem in the reader's own language: people are searching for
what they do, right now, and finding a competitor instead — because that
competitor's Google listing is more complete/active.

WHAT WE DO (3 items max)
1. We fix and complete your Google listing (translate: correct category,
   photos, hours, description)
2. We keep it active every week (translate: posts, review responses, so it
   looks alive to someone deciding whether to call)
3. We show you the numbers every month in plain language (translate: calls,
   direction requests, clicks — not "impressions" or "discovery metrics")

HOW IT WORKS (3 steps)
Free audit → we fix everything in [X] days → we manage it every month

PRICING (transparent, matches [FILL IN CURRENT VELLUMWIRE PRICING])

FINAL CTA
Low-commitment: "not sure if this is worth it? Let us look at your listing for
free, no pitch."

Rules: no em dashes, no generic SaaS-agency filler phrases, every claim must be
something we can actually back up (see the "what's confirmed" notes above) —
never invent a specific stat or promise a lead count.
```
