# Step 4 — Suspension Risk Protocol
> Human rules, not an AI prompt. Read before touching any live client profile. Primary source: Sterling Sky / Joy Hawkins (sterlingsky.ca/top-reasons-google-my-business-suspended-your-listing/) — the most cited agency specifically on GBP suspensions. All causes below are what Sterling Sky's own published playbook actually documents, not the fabricated percentage breakdown that appeared in an earlier, unverified draft of this plan (see root `README.md`, "What changed from the first draft").

---

## Why this is the most important file in this folder

A suspended profile can cost a client real revenue while it's down — plumbers/HVAC-scale estimates run into the thousands per month (one source estimates $8,000-15,000/month; treat that specific figure as a single-source, order-of-magnitude estimate, not a verified statistic). Worse: **if your own agency account gets flagged, every listing that account manages can be suspended at once.** This is confirmed and documented, not a theoretical risk. One careless edit doesn't just cost one client.

---

## Confirmed real causes of suspension (Sterling Sky's actual published list)

1. **Excessive edits** — too many changes, especially structural ones, in too short a time
2. **No physical presence at the listed address** — the address doesn't correspond to somewhere the business genuinely operates from or serves from
3. **The managing account itself gets suspended** — cascades to every profile it manages
4. **Duplicate listings** — the same business represented more than once
5. **High-scrutiny categories** — Google applies extra review to locksmiths, garage door services, and addiction/rehab treatment specifically. (Note: several of VellumWire's Tier-1 target niches — plumbing, electrical, HVAC — are adjacent home-service categories. Not on Google's highest-scrutiny list specifically, but close enough in shape that the same conservative editing discipline applies.)

No verified percentage breakdown of these causes exists — don't repeat or invent one.

## Account structure (non-negotiable)

```
[ ] Your agency account is always MANAGER. Never accept, request, or default into Owner.
[ ] The client keeps primary ownership on an account tied to their own business
    domain — not a personal Gmail.
[ ] Never use the same Google account for both Google Ads management and GBP
    management — an Ads policy violation can suspend the associated GBP profiles too.
[ ] If your agency account needs to make a public edit on Google Maps itself
    (not through the Business Profile dashboard), Sterling Sky's own practice is
    to use a separate, disposable account for this — a denied Maps edit can flag
    the account that submitted it, and you don't want that to be the account
    managing 10 client profiles.
```

## Editing discipline (non-negotiable)

```
[ ] Never make multiple large structural edits (name + address + category)
    on the same day, for the same profile — this combination specifically
    reads as a "hijacked listing" pattern to Google's automated review.
[ ] Space out structural changes: one significant structural change per
    profile per week, maximum.
[ ] Profile name = exact legal/signage name. Zero appended keywords, ever.
[ ] Never link a URL that redirects — a bare tracking/redirect link on the
    website field is a documented, automatic suspension trigger.
[ ] Remove any auto-generated services Google added that the client doesn't
    actually offer (a documented real case: a law firm was suspended over
    auto-generated "practice areas" it didn't actually practice — check the
    Services tab for anything Google added on its own, not just what you added).
[ ] Zero emojis, unusual characters, or unverifiable claims in any profile field.
```

## Reviews (non-negotiable)

```
[ ] Never buy reviews, incentivize them with a discount, or review-gate
    (asking only satisfied customers for a review, filtering out unhappy ones
    before they can post publicly, is itself a policy violation)
[ ] Never respond to a review aggressively on the client's behalf, even if
    the review is unfair
[ ] Report spam or fake third-party reviews through Google's official channel
    (support.google.com/business) — don't argue with them in public replies
```

## Client onboarding due diligence (see `0-briefing/0.1-client-intake-checklist.md` for the full version)

```
[ ] Address is real and receives mail — PO boxes and virtual offices are high risk
[ ] Existing profile name checked for keyword stuffing before any other work starts
[ ] No duplicate listings exist, or a plan is in place to resolve them first
[ ] Old agencies and inactive users removed from profile access
```

---

## If a suspension happens anyway

```
[ ] Do NOT edit the profile while it's under review — editing resets the clock
[ ] Do NOT create a new profile — this permanently loses reviews, history, and
    any ranking built up. The old profile, even suspended, is still worth
    fighting for.
[ ] File a single, well-documented appeal through the official channel:
    support.google.com/business/answer/4569145
[ ] Realistic timeline: 1-3 weeks typical, prepare the client for up to a month
[ ] After reinstatement: do not change multiple fields at once — that's exactly
    the pattern that can re-trigger review
```

---

## Why this is worth saying to a client directly

Most amateur competitors in this space don't have a suspension protocol at all. It's a legitimate differentiator: "your listing is a real business asset, and we manage it with a protocol specifically designed to never put it at risk" is true, specific, and something a competitor who's never thought about this can't credibly say.
